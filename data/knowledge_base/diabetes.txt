Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disease where blood sugar levels remain persistently high due to insufficient insulin production or ineffective insulin use.
Type 1 diabetes occurs when the immune system destroys insulin-producing cells — patients require lifelong insulin injections.
Type 2 diabetes is the most common form, often caused by obesity, poor diet, and physical inactivity — it can be managed and sometimes reversed with lifestyle changes.
Normal fasting blood glucose is 70-100 mg/dL. Pre-diabetes is 100-125 mg/dL. Diabetes is diagnosed at 126 mg/dL or above on two separate tests.
HbA1c test measures average blood sugar over 3 months. Below 5.7% is normal, 5.7-6.4% is pre-diabetes, 6.5% and above confirms diabetes.

Common symptoms of diabetes include frequent urination especially at night, excessive thirst, unexplained weight loss, constant fatigue, blurred vision, slow healing wounds, and tingling or numbness in hands and feet.
Many people have diabetes for years without symptoms — this is why regular blood sugar testing is critical especially after age 40 or if overweight.

Home management for diabetes: eat small frequent meals instead of large ones, replace white rice and white bread with brown rice, whole wheat roti, or oats.
Avoid sugary drinks like cola, packaged juices, and sweetened tea — drink plain water, unsweetened green tea, or karela juice instead.
Karela (bitter melon) juice taken on an empty stomach is a well-known home remedy in Pakistan that may help lower blood sugar naturally.
Methi (fenugreek) seeds soaked overnight in water and consumed in the morning on an empty stomach can help improve insulin sensitivity.
Jamun (black plum) seeds dried and powdered — half teaspoon twice daily — is a traditional remedy used for blood sugar control in South Asia.
Regular walking 30 minutes daily after meals is one of the most effective ways to control blood sugar without medication.

If diabetes is not treated or poorly controlled, it causes severe complications that affect every organ in the body.
Diabetic neuropathy: high blood sugar damages nerves causing burning pain, numbness and tingling in feet — can lead to foot ulcers that refuse to heal.
Diabetic nephropathy: kidneys filter blood; sustained high sugar damages kidney filters leading to chronic kidney disease and eventually kidney failure requiring dialysis.
Diabetic retinopathy: blood vessels in the eyes get damaged causing blurred vision and if untreated leads to complete blindness — a leading cause of blindness in Pakistan.
Cardiovascular disease: diabetics have 2-4 times higher risk of heart attack and stroke than non-diabetics.
Diabetic foot: poor circulation and nerve damage combined means small cuts or blisters on feet can develop into serious infections requiring amputation.

Seek immediate medical attention if blood sugar exceeds 300 mg/dL, or if patient experiences extreme confusion, rapid breathing, fruity breath odor, vomiting, or loss of consciousness — these are signs of diabetic ketoacidosis which is life-threatening.
Warning signs requiring urgent doctor visit: chest pain, sudden vision loss, inability to feel feet, wounds not healing after 2 weeks, high fever with uncontrolled sugar.

Pakistan-specific context: diabetes affects approximately 33 million Pakistanis making it one of the highest rates globally.
Free diabetes testing and basic medication is available at Basic Health Units (BHUs) and District Headquarter (DHQ) hospitals across Pakistan.
Insulin is available free of cost at most government hospitals — patients should never stop insulin without doctor supervision.
