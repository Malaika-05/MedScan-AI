Hypertension (high blood pressure) is called the silent killer because it causes no symptoms for years while silently damaging the heart, brain, kidneys, and blood vessels.
Normal blood pressure is below 120/80 mmHg. Elevated: 120-129/below 80. Stage 1 hypertension: 130-139/80-89. Stage 2 hypertension: 140/90 or above. Hypertensive crisis requiring emergency care: above 180/120.
The top number (systolic) is pressure when heart beats. The bottom number (diastolic) is pressure when heart rests between beats.
Pakistan has a hypertension epidemic — approximately 44% of adults above 40 have high blood pressure and 75% of them are undiagnosed or untreated.

Hypertension usually has no symptoms — many people feel completely fine while their blood pressure is dangerously high.
Occasional symptoms when BP is very high: severe headache especially at back of head in the morning, dizziness, blurred vision, nosebleeds, shortness of breath, chest discomfort, or pounding in chest or ears.

Long-term consequences of untreated hypertension are catastrophic.
Heart attack: consistently high pressure forces heart to work harder — heart muscle thickens and weakens eventually causing heart attack.
Stroke: high pressure can burst blood vessels in the brain causing hemorrhagic stroke, or cause blood clots blocking brain arteries causing ischemic stroke — leading cause of disability and death in Pakistan.
Kidney failure: high pressure damages the delicate filtering blood vessels in kidneys, progressively destroying kidney function requiring dialysis.
Vision loss: damaged blood vessels in the retina (hypertensive retinopathy) can cause permanent vision loss.
Aortic aneurysm: high pressure weakens major arteries causing dangerous bulging that can rupture — immediately fatal.

Home management of hypertension starts with diet.
DASH diet principles: reduce salt intake to less than 5 grams per day — stop adding salt at the table, reduce pickles (achar), papad, and processed foods.
Increase potassium-rich foods: bananas, oranges, spinach, potatoes, tomatoes — potassium helps lower blood pressure naturally.
Eat garlic daily — 2-3 raw cloves or in cooking — allicin in garlic has clinically proven blood pressure lowering effects.
Hibiscus (gurhal) tea: 2-3 cups daily of hibiscus flower tea can reduce systolic BP by 7-10 mmHg in some studies — a safe and pleasant remedy.
Reduce or eliminate ghee and fried foods — replace with olive oil or mustard oil in small quantities.
Completely avoid smoking and excessive tea consumption — nicotine and caffeine both raise blood pressure acutely.
Limit salt in cooking — use zeera (cumin), dhania (coriander), and spices for flavor instead of extra salt.

Lifestyle changes that genuinely work: brisk walking 30-40 minutes daily reduces BP by 5-8 mmHg.
Stress management through prayer, meditation, and adequate sleep 7-8 hours nightly significantly impacts blood pressure.
Weight loss — even 5kg reduction can lower systolic BP by 5 mmHg.

Never stop blood pressure medication without doctor advice even if you feel completely fine — BP medication controls but does not cure hypertension.
Take medication at the same time every day — consistency is critical. Missing doses causes dangerous BP spikes.
Monitor BP at home with a digital machine — check twice daily at rest, record results to show the doctor.

Hypertensive emergency — go to hospital immediately: BP above 180/120, severe headache, chest pain, shortness of breath, sudden vision changes, confusion, weakness on one side of body, difficulty speaking — these indicate imminent stroke or heart attack.
