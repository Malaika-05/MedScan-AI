Malaria is a life-threatening parasitic disease caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted to humans through the bite of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes.
Four species cause malaria in humans: Plasmodium falciparum (most dangerous), P. vivax (most common in Pakistan), P. malariae, and P. ovale.
Pakistan is a high-malaria-burden country especially in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Balochistan, Sindh, and tribal areas — highest transmission during and after monsoon season July to October.

Classic malaria symptoms appear in cycles: sudden onset of shivering and chills lasting 1-2 hours, followed by spiking fever 103-106F for 3-6 hours, then profuse sweating as fever breaks.
This fever cycle repeats every 48 hours in P. vivax malaria and every 72 hours in P. malariae malaria.
Other symptoms include severe headache, muscle and joint pain, nausea and vomiting, fatigue and weakness, and sometimes abdominal pain and diarrhea.
Severe falciparum malaria can cause altered consciousness, seizures, severe anemia, jaundice, kidney failure, and death if not treated within 24 hours.

Diagnosis: Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT) gives results in 15 minutes and is available at most health facilities. Blood smear microscopy is the gold standard.
Never self-medicate for malaria — treatment depends on the species and severity and wrong treatment causes drug resistance.

Home care while awaiting medical treatment: rest in a cool room, stay well hydrated with oral rehydration salts (ORS), water, or coconut water.
Apply cool wet cloth to forehead for fever — do not use ice directly on skin.
Paracetamol (Panadol) can reduce fever but never give aspirin to malaria patients especially children as it can cause dangerous bleeding.
Neem leaves boiled in water and used as steam inhalation is a traditional remedy to relieve fever symptoms — not a cure but provides comfort.
Tulsi (holy basil) leaves boiled with black pepper in water and consumed as tea may help reduce fever and boost immunity as a supportive measure.

Prevention is better than cure: sleep under insecticide-treated bed nets every night even in cities.
Use mosquito repellent creams or sprays on exposed skin especially at dawn and dusk when mosquitoes are most active.
Eliminate standing water around the home — mosquitoes breed in any still water including flower pots, old tires, and water storage tanks.
Wear full-sleeve shirts and long pants during evening hours especially in rural areas.

Seek immediate emergency care if: high fever with confusion or difficulty waking, seizures or convulsions, yellow eyes or skin (jaundice), very dark urine, inability to keep fluids down, pregnancy with fever, or any child under 5 with fever — children die rapidly from malaria.
Never wait more than 24 hours before going to a doctor if malaria is suspected.
